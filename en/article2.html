Women Scientists in Literature: Restoring the Voices of Those Erased

Women Scientists in Literature: Restoring the Voices of Those Erased

By Leila Tebrouri

4 min read.

While working on this project about the Matilda Effect, which I had never heard of before, I realized how much women in science have been erased from history and literature. The more I learned about it, the more I saw how many talented and passionate women were pushed aside. They were always there, but their names, their voices, and their discoveries slowly disappeared over time.

The invisibility of women in science stories

For centuries, most stories about science have been told from a man’s point of view. Women were often shown only as assistants, wives, or people who inspired men. That’s exactly what the Matilda Effect, explained by historian Margaret Rossiter, is about. It describes how women’s discoveries were often credited to men. When I learned that, I felt like a whole part of our shared history had been hidden, as if someone had chosen to erase it on purpose.

The forgotten women by Titiou Lecoq

This is also what Titiou Lecoq talks about in her book Les femmes oubliées (The Forgotten Women). She tells the stories of scientists, artists, writers, and politicians whose achievements were ignored or purposely forgotten. What I really like about her work is that she doesn’t just honor them. She also explains clearly how, over time, women’s successes were minimized or erased to fit a world built by men.

Literature as a mirror that needs fixing

I think literature has a huge role to play in changing that.It shapes how we see the world and who we look up to. If there are no female scientists in books, how can young girls imagine themselves in those roles? Too often, when women in science do appear, they’re shown as cold, shy, or a bit peculiar. Very few authors give them the depth and complexity they deserve. That’s why writers like Titiou Lecoq and Nikita Gill, with her book Fierce Fairy Tales, are so important today. They give women their voices back and show that they can be strong, emotional, curious, and brave all at once.

Bringing women scientists back to inspire the next generation.

Bringing women scientists back into literature isn’t just about correcting history. It’s about inspiring future generations and changing how we think about science. Every time a woman scientist is mentioned in a book or an article, it opens the door for new perspectives. What if, along with Einstein and Newton, we also talked about Lise Meitner, Rosalind Franklin, or Ada Lovelace? Their names deserve to be just as familiar as those of their male counterparts.
