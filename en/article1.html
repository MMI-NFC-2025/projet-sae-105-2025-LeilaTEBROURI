Nettie Stevens, Erased from History

Have you ever stopped to wonder how we actually know what decides whether a baby will be a boy or a girl?

By Leila Tebrouri

4 min read.

Most people would probably guess it was discovered by a famous scientist like Mendel or Thomas Hunt Morgan. But here's the twist the first person to uncover the answer wasn't either of them. It was a woman named Nettie Stevens (1861-1912), whose name has been left out of many history books. A short three-minute ARTE documentary, part of the series "Cherchez la femme!", tells her incredible story and how her discovery completely changed the future of genetics

Back When Genetics Was Just Beginning

At the end of the 19th century and the start of the 20th, genetics was still a very new and developing science. During this time, well-known scientists like Thomas Hunt Morgan and Edmund Beecher Wilson were seen as leaders in the field. You'd probably expect that one of them would have made the big discovery about how sex is determined.

But surprisingly, that breakthrough came from Nettie Stevens instead. She spent countless hours in the lab, looking carefully through a microscope and studying cells with incredible patience. Imagine this: a determined woman working in a world where science was almost entirely dominated by men, chasing an answer no one had found yet. And then, she finally made a life changing discovery.

The Discovery That Transformed Biology

Through her careful research, Nettie Stevens found something no one else had proven before:

  • Males have two different chromosomes what we now call XY
  • Females have two matching chromosomes known as XX

Today, this seems like basic biology we all learn in school but at the time, it was a completely new idea. Her work reshaped how scientists understood reproduction and inheritance, opening the door to what we now know as modern genetics.

When sexism takes recognition away

Unfortunately, as the documentary shows, Nettie Stevens didn't receive the credit she rightfully deserved. The scientific world back then and sadly, even now at times was deeply sexist. At first, some of her male colleagues dismissed her work, saying it wasn't that important. Later, they used her research in their own publications, taking nearly all the recognition for themselves.
