Women and Science: Restoring the Place of Female Researchers

Breaking stereotypes: a challenge that is still relevant today

By Leila Tebrouri

4 min read.

While working on a project about the Matilda Effect, I realized how persistent the idea is that women and science don’t belong together. In popular imagination, scientists are still mostly pictured as men. Women are often overlooked in universities, laboratories, and scientific publications. Learning about this made me understand something important: highlighting female researchers is not only about correcting history, it’s also about creating more equality in science.

GWI: a pioneering organization for women in science

To change this perception, women took action. In 1919, the International Federation of University Women was founded,which today is known as Graduate Women International (GWI). Created by visionary female researchers, the organization aimed to make women in science visible and prove that they fully belonged in the field. Over time, GWI became a major international player, especially between the two World Wars. It provided women with a space to connect, support each other, and gain recognition in areas where they were often invisible.

Research scholarships: a concrete step toward equality

One of GWI’s most important initiatives was creating international scholarships for women researchers. These grants allowed women to access the same opportunities as men: pursuing PhDs, traveling, publishing, and collaborating on major scientific projects. Thanks to these efforts, the image of women in science began to change. Women gained legitimacy, their contributions became more visible, and this was a crucial step toward professional and social recognition.

Linking the history of science with gender history

What fascinated me most is how this work connects the history of science with gender history. GWI didn’t just challenge institutional barriers; it also worked to change cultural perceptions of women scientists. This shows how stereotypes have shaped women’s role in research. Addressing these stereotypes is also a way to make science more fair, open, and inclusive.

Why this story still matters in this day and age

This history reminds us that science has never been only for men, and that women have always made important contributions, often behind the scenes. Organizations like GWI show that with determination, support networks, and solidarity, it is possible to transform women’s place in science. These stories remain vital today. They can inspire young girls to pursue scientific careers and believe in their abilities. The more women we see in research, the closer science gets to true equality.
